## Problem Statement

Clone the Jurassic Park Arcade experience (Raw Thrills, 2015 cabinet: 1–2 players, on-rails camera through jungle/river/vehicle sequences, crosshair shooting at dinosaurs, boss fights, medal/combo scoring) for tvOS on the builder's own Apple TV, reusing the BrawlerEngine lineage. The base snapshot is **MetalBrawler**: Metal render pipeline (true 3D perspective + look-at), ObjC++/C++ ECS, deterministic 120Hz fixed-tick sim (`kFixedDt = 1/120`), GameController input, macOS + tvOS app shells, AutoPilot headless CI, and the full skeletal animation stack. **Runtime shader compilation is ported in from MetalMoto** (`newLibraryWithSource` at launch, ~30 lines) — it exists only there, and neither repo has cgltf or a file watcher today; those are new work where the plan calls for them.

No light gun exists for Apple TV, so the aim model must be reinvented. The builder's two named risks going in: (1) getting/making dinosaur assets, (2) making aiming feel good without a light gun. The session resolved both: gyro aim is a documented, solved feel problem the platform supports but almost nobody uses; and the engine already has the skeletal animation pipeline the dinos need.

Third game on the engine. Standing workflow: Claude plans, Codex implements.

## What Makes This Cool

**Reading the animal IS the scoring system.** The identity in one line (from the Codex cold read): *"a playable nature-documentary disaster"* — each dinosaur has readable behavior tells, and the best players score because they understand the animal, not because they hose bullets into health bars.

Three pillars:

1. **Dino mastery scoring** — the builder's own answer to "score-chase or dino spectacle?" was *"It's hard to choose between B and C. I definitely want both."* This design makes that structural instead of contradictory: weak-point timing, attack-interrupt windows, tell-reads, no-miss tracking shots, and rare-behavior tags are simultaneously the spectacle (dinos behaving like animals) and the score depth (mastery of those behaviors). One system, not two budgets.
2. **Gyro aim as the resurrected light gun** — DualSense/DualShock 4 motion via GCMotion on tvOS, tuned to the gyro-as-mouse conventions documented at GyroWiki (Jibb Smart, gyrowiki.jibbsmart.com) and the Game Developer "Absolute Basics of Good Gyro Controls" article: stick coarse + gyro fine, click-to-recenter, tiered smoothing applied only near stillness, separate H/V sensitivity, no aim assist for gyro users. A rail shooter is the best possible genre for gyro: no camera control competes for the sensor — it's pure pointing. Almost no tvOS game does this.
3. **Levels are charts, not code** — a level is data: an authored rail spline + a spawn/event chart (like a rhythm-game chart), starting as dumb JSON and growing features only when a scene demands them. Reloadable during development (launch-time re-read first; a save-watcher is a later comfort feature). The deterministic sim makes every run a replay: score verification and headless AutoPilot CI follow cheaply — with the one caveat that async gyro samples must be latched/quantized into the 120Hz input log (explicit M4 scope, not a freebie).

## Constraints

- Apple ecosystem: tvOS lead platform, macOS daily dev target. Metal + ObjC++/C++, custom engine, no game engine.
- Gyro requires DualSense/DualShock 4 (the only pads exposing motion to tvOS; Xbox pads have no gyro, current Siri Remote motion is unreliable). Builder has not yet confirmed owning one — see The Assignment.
- Side project: milestones must be weekend-sized; aim feel gets tuned before content is built (MetalMoto's feel-first discipline).
- Assets: Quaternius CC0 animated low-poly dinos; graybox/stand-ins before that where useful; hero-asset upgrades (AI-generated or paid) only after the game is proven fun. Environment/set art (jungle, river, props, skybox) from CC0 nature packs (Quaternius Ultimate Nature, Kenney nature kits) — graybox corridors first, set dressing folded into M5's cut line.
- Original dino-island theme — no Jurassic Park names, logos, or music — so TestFlight distribution never hits an IP wall.

## Premises

1. **Pure rails.** The camera follows an authored spline with scripted look-at targets. No free movement, ever. A level = rail path + spawn/event chart (data, not code). **Chart events are keyed to distance along the rail**, not wall-clock time — pacing changes (scripted slowdowns, boss holds) then compose without re-timing every event; anything genuinely time-based (a delayed pounce after a trigger) hangs off a distance-keyed trigger. **Distance is arc-length, not raw spline parameter** — the loader precomputes an arc-length lookup table for each rail spline at chart-load time, so "distance along the rail" means constant physical distance regardless of how tight a curve is. Without this, naive spline-parameter travel makes the camera speed up through curves and crawl on straightaways, and an event authored at "distance 340" would land at a different real-world position depending on the curviness of everything before it.
2. **Gyro-first aim** (DualSense/DS4 via GCMotion, app manages `sensorsActive`): stick coarse, gyro fine, click-to-recenter, per-player sensitivity, per the GyroWiki gyro-as-mouse conventions. Fallback for non-gyro pads: stick aiming with **soft assist — reticle friction (reduced sensitivity while over a target) plus modest bullet magnetism; no hard snap-lock**. Aim feel gets a dedicated milestone before any content exists. **Aim model: a 2D screen-space reticle.** Gyro/stick input moves a screen-space crosshair only — the render camera never deviates from its scripted rail path (Premise 1), matching the cabinet's own light-gun-genre convention. Hit-testing is reticle-position vs. depth-tested on-screen dino bounds, not a ray-cast against a moving camera. (Considered and rejected: camera-look perturbation within a clamped cone — more immersive, but couples aim to the render camera and complicates "pure rails" and determinism.) **"No aim assist" is a starting hypothesis, not dogma** (outside-voice challenge, plan-eng-review): the GyroWiki convention comes from competitive-shooter communities calibrated for hardcore players; this game's actual audience is friends on a couch. M1 exists specifically to feel-test this — if pure no-assist feels bad for casual players, invisible stabilization or generous hit-boxes on fast targets are fair game to add, distinct from the hard snap-lock already rejected for the fallback path.
3. **Quaternius dino roster, stylized look everywhere.** The free CC0 Animated Low-Poly Dinosaurs pack (T-Rex, Velociraptor, Triceratops, Stegosaurus, Parasaurolophus, Apatosaurus; rigged with idle/walk/run/attack/jump/death clips; FBX+glTF) is the launch cast. Hero upgrades are a post-fun decision.
4. **(Revised in session)** Skeletal animation **already exists** in BrawlerEngine: GPU skinning shader (`Shaders/SkinnedMesh.metal`, 4 weights/vertex, 64-bone palette), `CharacterLoader` baking clips to 30fps bone-matrix palettes with lerped sampling, `AnimationComponent`/`AnimationSystem` with cross-fade blending. The baked-matrix approach is rig-agnostic, so this is not new construction — but it is not a free weekend either: it's per-species clip tables (replacing the single global `AnimClipID` enum, whose fragility the header itself documents at four update sites, not three — including a currently-silent footgun: "missing entries aggregate-initialize to 0 (a zero-length clip with no hitbox) — silently"), plus an asset-ingestion decision for Quaternius's layout (see Open Question 2 — neither engine currently has cgltf or any glTF path; the realistic options are extending `CharacterLoader`'s existing ModelIO/FBX→USDZ flow, or newly integrating cgltf from scratch). **The per-species replacement must not inherit the silent-zero-fill footgun**: M3 adds load-time validation that fails loudly (assertion or error log) when a species' required clip set is incomplete, rather than silently playing a zero-length clip — with 6 species × 6-8 clips each, this converts dozens of potential silent bugs (e.g. a missing raptor "interrupt" clip quietly breaking the interrupt-window mechanic that IS the scoring identity) into one obvious load-time error. Budget the overall clip-table work as the second-riskiest milestone after aim feel (M1), not a freebie. **ozz-animation is not needed** (Codex recommended it before this fact was checked).
5. **Engine reuse = vendored snapshot of BrawlerEngine**, same recipe as MetalMoto. No shared-engine extraction project — three games makes it tempting; it is still a side-project trap.
6. **(Revised in session)** **Both 1-player and 2-player co-op modes are first-class from the start screen** (like the cabinet's 1P/2P buttons). Arcade scoring — accuracy medals, combo streaks, weak-point bonuses — is the replay loop in both modes. **Data model, not gameplay, is 2P-shaped from M1** (outside-voice challenge, plan-eng-review): "first-class from the start" and "M1 is 1P-only" are reconciled by keeping M1's *gameplay* single-player (the go/no-go question — does aim feel good — doesn't need two players to answer) while designing M1's data structures (reticle state, score, health) as arrays/maps over player-slot from the first line of code, not a single-player-shaped struct retrofitted later. Zero added M1 scope; avoids a real refactor at M5.
7. **Distribution: builder's Apple TV (dev-signed) + TestFlight for friends, original theme.** Deterministic fixed-tick sim preserved untouched → score-verification replays and headless AutoPilot CI. (Ghost ships — racing a recorded run — were floated in early discussion but add near-zero value in a rail shooter where every player already sees the same scripted path; cut, not milestoned.)
8. **Player has a health/fail state.** Uninterrupted attacks and unblocked hits drain player health (cabinet-style); reaching zero ends that player's run early with a game-over grade rather than always reaching the end. This gives the interrupt-window mechanic real stakes — see M3/M4. **In 2P, health is per-player** (cabinet norm: separate lives): a depleted player sits out (spectates, reticle hidden) while their partner continues solo; the run ends when both are out or the act completes.

## System Tick Ordering

`World.mm`'s fixed-tick loop is a hardcoded linear sequence of `System_update` calls (verified: `InputSystem_update` → ... → `PhysicsSystem_update` → ... → `AnimationSystem_update` → ... → `CombatSystem_update` → ...) — there's no plugin/registration mechanism, so new systems must be manually inserted at the right position. This design adds six new systems; their required relative order:

1. `GyroInputSystem_update` — alongside/after `InputSystem_update` (reads raw controller + motion state)
2. `ReticleSystem_update` — after gyro/stick input, before hit-testing (produces the current screen-space reticle position consumed by everything below)
3. `RailCameraSystem_update` — independent of aim (Premise 1: camera never reacts to aim), can run anywhere before rendering; natural slot alongside existing camera/physics update
4. `DinoBehaviorSystem_update` — after `RailCameraSystem_update` (dinos need current camera-relative visibility/staging), before scoring (produces tell/attack/interrupt state that scoring reads), likely near where `EnemyAISystem_update` sits today
5. `ScoringSystem_update` — after `DinoBehaviorSystem_update` and after hit-testing resolves (reads this tick's hits + behavior state), analogous position to `CombatSystem_update`
6. `HealthSystem_update` — after `ScoringSystem_update` (reads this tick's unblocked/interrupted-attack outcomes to apply damage), before `AnimationSystem_update` (death/damage-react clips need the resulting health state)

This ordering is a starting hypothesis to validate during M1-M3, not a final spec — but it must be written down before implementation starts rather than inferred from the existing 20-entry list, to avoid stale-by-one-tick bugs (a system reading last tick's data because it landed in the wrong slot).

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Proving Slice → Grow Linearly (minimal viable)
Codex's weekend slice, trimmed: vendored engine, gyro reticle system, one 60–90s jungle rail, stand-in dinos, basic hit/score, headless replay test. Content grows scene by scene, ad hoc.
Effort: S per milestone (human ~2 weekends to first playable / CC: days). Risk: Low.
Pros: fastest answer to the killer question (gyro feel on the actual TV); every milestone playable; purest MetalMoto graybox discipline.
Cons: choreography tends to get hardcoded in C++ and later fights the data-driven model the game eventually needs; scoring depth arrives late.
Reuses: BrawlerEngine render/ECS/tick/input/animation/AutoPilot as-is; MetalMoto's vendoring recipe.

### Approach B: Chart-Driven Content Engine (ideal architecture)
Authoring spine first: declarative hot-reloadable level format, encounter system with behavior tells, tooling; then author 3 acts + boss on top. Levels become data authoring, not programming.
Effort: L (human ~2 months of weekends / CC: ~2–3 weeks of sessions). Risk: Med.
Pros: content velocity — the real long-term bottleneck for rail shooters — solved structurally; hot-reload charts extend the runtime-shader-compilation workflow to gameplay.
Cons: weeks of pipeline before anything is fun (the classic side-project stall); aim feel, the go/no-go risk, validated last; its biggest line item (animation pipeline) evaporated when Premise 4 was corrected.
Reuses: everything A reuses, plus the hot-reload ethos.

### Approach C: Dino Mastery Arcade (creative/lateral — CHOSEN)
A's fun-first build order + B's data format from day one in minimal form (a dumb JSON chart that grows only when a scene demands it), aimed at the Codex identity: every dino has readable tells and interruptible attacks; scoring rewards understanding the animal.
Effort: M (human ~3–5 weekends to a shareable act / CC: ~1–2 weeks of sessions — reduced by the Premise 4 correction). Risk: Low-Med.
Pros: resolves "I want both" structurally — spectacle IS the scoring system; aim feel is still milestone one; avoids A's hardcoded-content trap without B's tooling stall; behavior tells are animation-clip choreography, expressible with the Quaternius clip set.
Cons: the behavior/tell system needs real design iteration beyond cloning the cabinet; rare-behavior content is easy to over-scope.
Reuses: everything A reuses + Quaternius clips as the tell vocabulary.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach C: Dino Mastery Arcade** — approved by the builder in session. Rationale: the builder's core-fantasy answer ("I definitely want both") is a contradiction under A or B and *is the design* under C; with skeletal animation already in the engine, C costs roughly what A does, and the only genuinely new engine systems are gyro aim and the rail/chart system.

## Open Questions

1. **Controller inventory:** does the builder own a DualSense/DualShock 4? (Gyro is PlayStation-only on tvOS.) If not: buy two.
2a. **Bone-count verification (pre-M3, 10 min):** the GPU skinning shader caps at `kMaxBones = 64` (`Shaders/SkinnedMesh.metal`). Before M3 begins, manually check the Quaternius dino rigs' bone counts against this cap (open one in Blender or inspect the glTF/FBX skeleton) — low-poly rigs are almost always well under 64, but this hasn't been verified, and if one species exceeds it, that's a mid-milestone asset re-rig, not a code fix. **Bone count is one risk among several** (outside-voice challenge, plan-eng-review): also check clip naming consistency across species, retargeting behavior (does the loader assume a fixed skeleton shape?), scale/origin/pivot consistency, and material/texture format — during the same pre-M3 asset-inspection pass, not just bone count in isolation.
2. **Asset ingestion path:** extend `CharacterLoader`'s existing ModelIO/FBX→USDZ conversion flow to Quaternius's layout (clips embedded in one file, vs. Mixamo's file-per-clip), or newly integrate cgltf and write a glTF skinned-mesh/clip importer from scratch (cgltf is not currently a dependency of either engine)? Decide when the first dino is imported; prefer whichever needs less new code.
3. **Clip-table refactor scope:** how far to generalize the global `AnimClipID` enum (currently update-in-three-places fragile) into per-species clip tables — minimal now vs. clean now.
4. **"Don't-shoot" targets:** does scoring include protected targets (herbivores, NPC vehicles) whose hits cost points? Strong flavor for the mastery identity; adds choreography cost. Decide during Act 1 design.
5. **Weapon model:** unlimited machine gun (cabinet-style) vs. reload rhythm; special-weapon pickups. Defer to feel testing in M1.
6. **Game title:** MetalRex is a placeholder. Codex's "Gyro Safari Crisis" energy, Metal* lineage, and the original-theme constraint should inform the real name.
7. **Audio:** original theme needs original (or CC0) music and dino SFX; sourcing not yet planned.
8. **2P semantics:** shared full screen with two reticles (cabinet-style, not split-screen) is the assumption. Scoring is **per-player** (separate accuracy/streak/medals, cabinet-style competitive-cooperative tension), with a combined end-of-run grade shown alongside individual grades. Confirm during M5b.
9. **Controller disconnect mid-run:** `Brawler-tvOS/GameViewController.mm` already assigns up to 4 connected GCControllers to player slots and handles connect/disconnect notifications, but this design doesn't yet specify player-facing behavior when a controller (especially a DualSense mid-gyro-aim) drops during an encounter — a real scenario on tvOS Bluetooth, not just an edge case. Leading option: 1P pauses and waits for reconnect; in 2P, the affected player sits out (reticle hidden) while the other continues, matching cabinet convention that one player leaving doesn't end the other's game. Decide during M5b implementation.

## Success Criteria

- **The go/no-go (end of M1, 1P only):** someone picks up the controller and is hitting what they point at within 10 seconds, no explanation needed; gyro reticle feels like pointing, not steering.
- **The Codex criterion (end of M5a, solo):** friends who play a full act ask for another run without being prompted.
- Both 1P and 2P are selectable from the start screen (from M5b onward) and complete a full act.
- A full act runs 3–5 minutes, can end early on player-health-depleted, and always ends with a per-player arcade-style grade screen (accuracy, streaks, weak-point medals).
- Determinism holds: a recorded input log replayed headlessly (AutoPilot) reproduces the identical hit/score timeline, enforced in CI.
- Level content lives in chart files; adding a spawn beat requires no C++ change.

## Worktree Parallelization Strategy

Sequential implementation, no parallelization opportunity. M0 (engine vendor, incl. the GCMotion spike) blocks everything; M1 (aim feel) is a hard go/no-go gate before content work starts; M2 (rail/chart) and M3 (dinos) both depend on M0 and are natural single-threaded successors since M3's encounters need M2's chart format to exist; M4a/M4b depend on M1-M3 producing real hits/scores to verify against; M5a (solo content) integrates M1-M4, and M5b (co-op join) is a thin UI layer on top of M5a's already-2P-shaped data. This is a single small engine + single game loop, not a multi-service system — module boundaries don't create independent lanes here.
